The history of American religious life in the twentieth century is marked by a significant shift toward the therapeutic and the pragmatic, a transition led by figures who sought to reconcile traditional Protestantism with the demands of a modern, consumerist society. At the forefront of this movement was Dr. Norman Vincent Peale, the longtime pastor of Marble Collegiate Church in Manhattan and the author of the era-defining text, *The Power of Positive Thinking*. Peale’s ministry represented a fusion of theological simplification, psychological optimism, and a belief in the causal power of the human mind to shape external reality. This ideology did more than provide a comforting message to a postwar generation; it served as the foundational religious and psychological framework for one of his most prominent and faithful congregants, Donald J. Trump. The impact of Peale’s ideology on Trump is not merely a biographical detail but a central mechanism for understanding Trump’s views on religion, success, and the nature of truth. By synthesizing Peale’s biography, the tenets of "Positive Thinking," and the specific historical interactions between the Peale and Trump families, a clear narrative emerges of a religious worldview that replaces the traditional concepts of sin and repentance with the imperatives of confidence and victory.

## **The Formative Years and Professional Ascent of Norman Vincent Peale**

Norman Vincent Peale was born in 1898 in the small town of Bowersville, Ohio, into a world shaped by the modest pieties of Midwestern Methodism.1 His father was a Methodist minister, and as a boy, Peale participated in the typical labors of the era, delivering newspapers and working in a local grocery store.1 These early experiences instilled in him a respect for the "self-made" archetype, even as he struggled personally with a debilitating sense of inferiority and self-doubt.3 Peale’s academic journey took him to Ohio Wesleyan University and subsequently to the Boston University School of Theology, where he was ordained as a Methodist minister in 1922\.1

Peale’s early pastorates were served within the Methodist Episcopal Church, but his career was fundamentally transformed in 1932 when he changed his affiliation to the Reformed Church in America to accept the pastorate at Marble Collegiate Church in New York City.2 Marble Collegiate, founded in 1628, offered a prestigious and influential platform on Fifth Avenue, providing Peale with access to the city’s business and political elite.4 Over the next fifty-two years, Peale would grow the congregation from a mere 600 members to over 5,000, turning the church into a destination for both regular parishioners and tourists seeking his "spirited sermons".5

Peale was a pioneer in the use of mass media for religious outreach. His weekly radio program, "The Art of Living," and his television appearances made him a household name long before the rise of the modern televangelist.4 Alongside his wife, Ruth Stafford Peale, he founded *Guideposts* magazine, which reached the largest circulation of any religious periodical in the United States by the 1950s.4 Through these platforms, Peale disseminated a message of optimism and faith that he argued was "down-to-earth" and "practical," focusing on the individual's ability to find confidence and inner peace through God's love.6

## **The Ideology of Positive Thinking: A Theological and Psychological Synthesis**

The core of Peale’s ideology was established in his 1952 classic, *The Power of Positive Thinking*. The book’s central premise is that faith in oneself and in God creates a psychological state that allows "good things" to happen.7 Peale’s first sentence—"Believe in yourself"—served as a summary of his life’s work.4 He argued that negative thoughts, self-doubt, and worry were the primary obstacles to a successful and happy life, and that these could be overcome through a mixture of prayerful exercises and psychological conditioning.

### **The Influence of the New Thought Movement**

Peale’s ideas were not entirely original but were deeply rooted in the nineteenth-century "New Thought" or "Mind Cure" movement.8 New Thought, pioneered by figures like Phineas Parkhurst Quimby, posited that ideas and thoughts shape reality and that the spiritual realm gives form to the material world.8 This movement emphasized that disease, unhappiness, and misfortune were the direct result of "incorrect thinking," and that "correct thinking"—defined as faith in a universal divine intelligence—could heal any ill and transform any situation.8

Peale effectively Christianized these metaphysical principles, replacing the esoteric terminology of New Thought with biblical language and Protestant aesthetics. He viewed God less as a divine judge and more as a "benign power" or a "vital force" that individuals could "tap" for personal success.3 This transformation of God into a source of energy for the individual was a hallmark of what critics called "religio-psychiatry," a term Peale himself embraced as he co-founded a clinic for pastoral psychology.7

Peale’s theology was fundamentally pragmatic. He stated that Christianity was not a "creed to be recited" but a "power to be tapped".3 He promised that those who followed his techniques would have "constant energy" and would be able to "modify or overcome" any fact facing them. This approach aimed to reconcile liberal Protestantism with the affluent society of the 1950s, assuring readers that material success was a defensible Christian goal.13

## **The Integration of the Trump Family at Marble Collegiate Church**

The relationship between the Trump family and Norman Vincent Peale began in the 1970s when Fred and Mary Anne Trump moved from the First Presbyterian Church in Jamaica, Queens, to Marble Collegiate Church in Manhattan.14 Fred Trump, a successful real estate developer, was "immediately drawn" to Peale’s teachings.5 The elder Trump found a spiritual validation for his own business acumen in Peale’s sermons, which often featured anecdotes of executives overcoming odds through tenacity and faith.15 Peale’s message that success was a sign of divine favor resonated with a man who had built a multi-million-dollar empire through sheer confidence.16

Donald Trump attended Marble Collegiate throughout his young adulthood and remained a member for over fifty years, citing Peale as a formative mentor and a "wonderful person".14 The church was the site of the most significant events in the family’s life: Trump and his two sisters, Maryanne and Elizabeth, were married there, and Peale personally officiated Trump’s first wedding to Ivana Zelníčková in 1977\.17 In 1988, Trump even served as a co-host for Peale’s 90th birthday celebration at the Waldorf Astoria. At Peale's funeral in 1993, Trump was a prominent attendee alongside figures like Richard Nixon.16

### **The Paternal Legacy of Positive Thinking**

The impact of Peale on Donald Trump was reinforced by his father’s own "proto-Peale" mindset. Long before Peale codified his canon, Donald's grandfather, Friedrich, had used similar levels of confidence and tenacity during the Klondike gold rush, and his father, Fred, had deployed these traits to build his real estate fortune.16 Thus, Peale’s preaching served as a religious confirmation of the Trump family's existing cultural values.

Trump frequently highlighted the mutual respect between himself and the minister, claiming that Peale thought he was his "greatest student of all time".12 This relationship moved beyond typical pastor-parishioner dynamics to a form of ideological discipleship. Peale, in turn, described Trump as one of America's "top positive thinkers" and predicted he would be the "greatest builder of our time".14

## **Peale’s Impact on Trump’s View of Religion: The Rejection of Repentance**

One of the most profound ways Peale’s ideology impacted Donald Trump’s religious view is in the virtual elimination of the concept of human sin and the necessity of repentance. In traditional Christianity, the acknowledgment of one's own weakness and the seeking of divine forgiveness are central to the faith.12 However, Peale’s framework suggested that acknowledging weakness or sin was "injurious to spiritual health" because it sapped the "vital force" necessary for success.12

### **The Theological Conflict**

Trump’s famous comment in 2015 that he had "never asked God for forgiveness" is the ultimate expression of the Peale-conditioned mind.12 In Peale’s system, there is no place for human frailty; dependency and sinfulness must be overcome by a "true estimate" of one's ability, raised by ten percent.9 This creates a "self-sufficient faith" that views the heart not as "desperately wicked," as described in the Bible, but as "bathed in California sunshine".12

Trump's identification with "An Eye for an Eye" as his favorite biblical verse further highlights a departure from traditional New Testament themes of mercy and forgiveness, focusing instead on a more combative stance. Critics argue that a man who does not need to ask for forgiveness may find it difficult to forgive others, leading to a worldview that "mocks and reviles every sign of weakness in his fellow men".12

## **The Business of Belief: *The Art of the Deal* and Positive Thinking**

The influence of Peale’s ideology is perhaps most explicitly stated in Trump’s 1987 book, *The Art of the Deal*. The book contains an 11-step formula for business success that Trump acknowledges was inspired by Peale’s *The Power of Positive Thinking*.18 These steps are designed to cultivate an "unfailing self-confidence" in the face of challenges.19

### **Analysis of the 11-Step Success Formula**

Trump’s business strategies, such as "Thinking Big" and using "Truthful Hyperbole," are direct applications of Peale’s concepts of "imaging" and "attitudes over facts".9 If Peale taught that a "confident and optimistic thought pattern can modify or overcome the fact altogether," Trump applied this to real estate by creating perceptions of exclusivity and scarcity, thereby increasing the value of his offers.

One notable case study is Trump’s purchase of Mar-a-Lago. When his initial offers were rejected, he purchased the beachfront property between the mansion and the ocean and threatened to build a housing development that would ruin the view.19 This aggressive use of "leverage" was paired with a "dream big" approach that allowed him to visualize the property as his long before the deal was closed.19

### **Persistence During Financial Crises**

The most significant test of Peale’s impact occurred during the early 1990s when Trump’s businesses faced severe financial distress and bankruptcies. Trump credited Peale’s book with helping him maintain an optimistic attitude, stating he "refused to give in to the negative circumstances" even when he was over $1 billion in debt. He famously pointed out a homeless man to his daughter, remarking that the man had "a billion dollars more than me" because he lacked the debt burden—yet Trump refused to entertain "failure thoughts". By projecting enough confidence to negotiate with bankers—telling them "if I have a problem, then you have a problem"—he negotiated his way out of debt, a process he described as emerging on a "very victorious level".

## **Political Precedents: Peale’s 1960 Meeting and Religious Nationalism**

Peale’s impact on Trump’s worldview extended into the realm of politics and the relationship between church and state. Although Peale often argued against the involvement of clergy in politics, he had controversial affiliations with politically active organizations as early as the late 1930s.20

### **The Citizens for Religious Freedom Meeting**

A pivotal moment in Peale’s political life occurred in September 1960 when he led a group of 150 Protestant clergymen, calling themselves the "Citizens for Religious Freedom".21 The group met to oppose the election of John F. Kennedy, the nation’s first Catholic presidential nominee, warning that American culture was "at stake" and that a Catholic president would be beholden to the Vatican.21

This event highlights a specific form of "Pro-Christian nationalism" that Peale advocated—the idea that America is fundamentally a Protestant nation that must be protected from external "godless" or foreign influences.22 This aligns with Trump’s later political strategy of positioning himself as the "protector" of Christianity and his use of "religious nationalism" to define national success.22 Both Peale and Trump equated commercial acumen and national strength with piety, framing failure as a result of "collectivism" or "godlessness".22

## **The Evolution of Influence: From Marble Collegiate to Paula White**

As Trump transitioned to the presidency, his religious affiliations shifted toward the charismatic and Pentecostal traditions, yet they remained anchored in the principles learned from Peale. This is most clearly seen in his long association with Paula White-Cain, a prominent proponent of "Prosperity Theology".14

### **The Link to Prosperity Gospel**

Peale’s theology is considered the direct ancestor of the modern Prosperity Gospel.3 While Peale focused heavily on mental health, the Prosperity Gospel extended this logic to claim that God wants believers to be rich and healthy.3 Both share a pragmatic view of faith: God is a power to be "tapped" for material benefit.3 Trump was drawn to White's "pragmatic and informal" approach, which emphasized overcoming demonic influences and achieving one's purpose.14

## **The Impact on Truth: "Alternative Facts" and the Reality Distortion Field**

A final insight into Peale’s impact involves their shared "eccentric orientation to truth".9 Because Peale taught that "attitudes are more important than facts," he provided a moral foundation for what would later be called "alternative facts".9 In this worldview, a "little exaggeration" is not a lie but a form of "picturing success" that is morally endorsed.9

### **Reframing Failure and Defeat**

This "reality distortion field" is visible in Trump’s persistent refusal to acknowledge facts that suggest failure, such as his claims about inaugural audience sizes or his refusal to concede the 2020 election.9 If Peale taught that "the way you think about a fact may defeat you before you ever do anything about it," then Trump’s refusal to concede is a perfect enactment of the rule: "Never entertain a failure thought".16 For a Peale-conditioned mind, acknowledging defeat would be a "repudiation of the core message" of his life’s philosophy.16

### **Divine Protection and the Butler Assassination Attempt**

Trump’s response to the 2024 assassination attempt in Butler, Pennsylvania, further reflects this "positive imaging" of divine favor. He described his survival—attributed to a momentary head tilt—as being "by luck or by God". He stated on social media that it was "God alone who prevented the unthinkable from happening," framing his survival as a sign that he is a "chosen" figure, echoing the Peale-esque idea that God provides "constant energy" and protection to those who believe in their own success.

## **Conclusion: The Persistent Legacy of Positive Thinking in American Power**

Norman Vincent Peale was the architect of a religious ideology that fundamentally altered the American Protestant landscape, replacing traditional focus on sin with a buoyant, "me-first" focus on success and personal energy.23 This ideology provided Donald Trump with a cohesive religious worldview that validates his business tactics, supports his political nationalism, and justifies his rejection of traditional moral categories like repentance and human frailty.12

The impact of Peale on Trump is absolute: it provided the "moral foundation" for his interpretation of truth, his disdain for "weakness," and his relentless optimism.9 While Peale’s family, including his son John Peale, has expressed that they "cringe" when Trump invokes his name, Trump continues to define his Christianity through the lens of "being a winner". As Trump weaves religious nationalism into his campaigns—even selling "God Bless the USA" Bibles—he fulfills the legacy of a mentor who believed that faith was not a creed to be recited, but a power to be tapped for the dominance of the self and the nation.22
